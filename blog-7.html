Understanding Check Engine Lights: Common Causes and Fixes

Don't Panic

The check engine light (CEL) can be alarming, but it doesn't always indicate a major problem. Understanding what triggers it helps you respond appropriately.

How It Works

Your car's computer (ECU) monitors hundreds of sensors. When readings fall outside normal parameters, it stores a diagnostic trouble code (DTC) and illuminates the CEL.

Common Causes

Loose Gas Cap: The most common cause. Tighten or replace the cap and the light should clear within a few drive cycles.

Oxygen Sensor: Monitors exhaust gases. A failing O2 sensor reduces fuel economy but won't leave you stranded. Replacement cost: $200-$400.

Catalytic Converter: Reduces emissions. Failure can be expensive ($1,000-$2,500) but is often caused by neglecting other issues.

Mass Airflow Sensor: Measures air entering the engine. Failure affects performance and fuel economy. Cost: $200-$400.

What to Do

If the light is steady, get it checked within a few days. If it's flashing, pull over immediately - this indicates a serious issue like misfiring that can damage the catalytic converter.

Reading Codes

Use an OBD-II scanner to read codes yourself. Basic scanners cost $20-$50 and can save you diagnostic fees. Many auto parts stores also offer free code reading.
